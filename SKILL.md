# review-scientific-paper
Review scientific manuscripts and produce structured peer-review reports. Use when the user asks to review, critically assess, pre-review, or provide reviewer comments on a research manuscript, preprint, thesis chapter, or scientific paper.

# Core principles
- Evaluate claims only against evidence present in the manuscript.
- Do not invent missing experiments, results, citations, or journal requirements.
- Distinguish confirmed problems from questions and uncertainties.
- Refer to specific sections, figures, tables, equations, or page numbers whenever possible.
- Prioritize scientific validity over wording and formatting.
- Explain why each major concern matters and what evidence could resolve it.
- Do not recommend additional experiments merely because they would be interesting.

# Workflow
1. Identify the manuscript type, research question, main claims, methods, and intended contribution.
2. State any missing materials that limit the review, such as supplementary files, raw data, code, or reporting checklists.
3. Build a claim–evidence map connecting each central conclusion to its supporting results.
4. Evaluate:
   - novelty and significance;
   - study design and controls;
   - methods and reproducibility;
   - statistical validity;
   - consistency between results and conclusions;
   - figures, tables, and data presentation;
   - alternative explanations and limitations;
   - ethics, data availability, code availability, and reporting quality;
   - relevant literature coverage.
5. Separate findings into major and minor concerns.
6. Check that every criticism is specific, evidence-grounded, and actionable.
7. Produce the report using `references/report-template.md`.

# Reference routing
- Read `references/review-criteria.md` for the complete assessment criteria.
- Read `references/statistics-checklist.md` when the manuscript contains quantitative analyses, hypothesis testing, machine-learning evaluation, or statistical claims.
- Read `references/report-template.md` before producing the final report.

# Recommendation rules
- Do not base the recommendation on writing quality alone.
- Treat unsupported central conclusions, invalid study design, data integrity concerns, and irreparable methodological problems as potentially decisive.
- State confidence in the recommendation and explain any limitations caused by missing information.
- When giving a final opinion, the journal's reputation in the field, impact factor, and other factors should also be considered.
