## 1. General Review Principles

- Evaluate only what is presented in the manuscript and accompanying materials.
- Prioritize scientific validity over language, formatting, and presentation.
- Distinguish demonstrated problems, reasonable concerns, unresolved questions, and optional suggestions.
- Link each criticism to a specific section, page, figure, table, equation, or claim whenever possible.
- Explain why each issue matters and which conclusion it affects.
- Request additional analyses, explanations, or experiments only when they are necessary to evaluate a central claim.
- Do not request experiments merely because they would be interesting.
- Do not present personal preferences as disciplinary standards or journal requirements.
- State explicitly when missing information prevents a reliable judgment.
- Treat novelty, priority, and literature-coverage claims cautiously unless they have been independently verified.

## 2. Severity Classification

### 2.1 Fundamental Concern

Use this classification only when an issue may invalidate the central conclusions and cannot reasonably be resolved through ordinary revision.

Examples include:

- The study design cannot answer the central research question.
- Essential controls are absent, preventing attribution of the reported effect.
- The available evidence does not support the primary conclusion.
- A central method contains an irreparable systematic bias.
- The main results cannot be verified from the reported data, analysis, or records.
- A serious research-integrity or ethical concern requires editorial investigation.

Do not use this classification casually. Explain the evidence, consequences, and remaining uncertainty.

### 2.2 Major Concern

Use this classification for issues that materially affect validity, interpretation, reproducibility, or confidence but may be resolvable through revision.

Examples include:

- Key methodological details are insufficient to assess validity or reproduce the work.
- Sample selection, controls, or experimental procedures create a substantial risk of bias.
- The statistical model, validation method, or evaluation metric is unsuitable.
- The abstract or discussion makes causal, mechanistic, or general claims unsupported by the results.
- Important confounders, alternative explanations, or sensitivity analyses are not addressed.
- Figures or supplementary materials omit information needed to evaluate a central conclusion.
- Important negative results, exclusions, or failed experiments are not explained.
- The distinction between the claimed contribution and prior work is not established.

### 2.3 Minor Concern

Use this classification for localized issues unlikely to alter the main conclusions but relevant to clarity, completeness, or reproducibility.

Examples include:

- A limited methodological detail is missing.
- A figure legend, unit, abbreviation, axis, error bar, or sample size is unclear.
- Terminology is inaccurate or inconsistent.
- Minor inconsistencies exist between the text, figures, tables, or supplementary materials.
- A localized statement is ambiguous or overstated.
- Citation coverage is incomplete without undermining the central novelty claim.
- Language or organization interferes with comprehension.

### 2.4 Question or Optional Suggestion

Label the following as questions or suggestions rather than scientific deficiencies:

- Points requiring clarification but not yet demonstrated to be incorrect.
- Analyses that could improve the paper but are not necessary for publication.
- Preferences concerning writing style, organization, or visual presentation.
- New research directions outside the manuscript’s stated scope.

## 3. Core Assessment Dimensions

### 3.1 Research Question and Contribution

Assess whether:

- The research question is clear, important, and testable.
- The objectives are consistent across the abstract, introduction, methods, and discussion.
- The theoretical, methodological, or practical contribution is defined precisely.
- The contribution goes beyond applying an existing method to a different dataset or setting.
- The claimed significance is supported by the results rather than rhetorical framing.

When raising novelty concerns:

- Identify the category of prior work that may overlap with the manuscript.
- Distinguish missing citations from a genuine lack of novelty.
- Use cautious language when literature verification has not been performed.
- Never require authors to cite the reviewer’s own work without a clear scientific reason.

### 3.2 Claims and Supporting Evidence

For every central claim, determine:

1. What exactly is being claimed?
2. Which experiment, analysis, figure, or table supports it?
3. Does the evidence directly address the claim?
4. Are there plausible alternative explanations?
5. Is the strength of the claim proportional to the strength of the evidence?
6. Does the conclusion depend on unreported or untested assumptions?

Check specifically for:

- Inferring causation from correlation.
- Generalizing from one model, population, location, or dataset.
- Treating statistical significance as practical importance.
- Treating predictive accuracy as evidence of mechanism.
- Extending in vitro, animal, or simulated findings directly to clinical or real-world outcomes.
- Treating failure to reject a null hypothesis as proof of equivalence.
- Treating absence of evidence as evidence of absence.

### 3.3 Study Design

Assess whether:

- The design is appropriate for the research question.
- Experimental groups, control groups, and baselines are suitable.
- Inclusion, exclusion, assignment, and stopping rules are clear.
- Randomization, blinding, matching, or balancing procedures are appropriate.
- Sample selection may introduce selection bias.
- Repeated measures, hierarchical structures, batch effects, or site effects are handled correctly.
- Technical replicates are distinguished from biological or independent replicates.
- Pseudoreplication, data leakage, or train–test contamination is present.
- Important confounding variables are identified and controlled.
- Temporal ordering supports any causal or mechanistic interpretation.
- Positive and negative controls are sufficient.
- Internal and external validity are appropriate for the stated conclusions.

### 3.4 Methods and Reproducibility

Assess whether:

- The methods contain enough detail for a qualified researcher to reproduce the work.
- Sample sizes, experiment counts, and independent replication are reported.
- Instruments, reagents, software, databases, and versions are identified.
- Parameters, thresholds, preprocessing steps, and exclusion rules are documented.
- Methods are applied consistently across experiments.
- Protocol deviations and methodological changes are disclosed.
- Custom methods or algorithms are adequately validated.
- Data-processing decisions may introduce bias.
- Essential methodological information is hidden in unavailable supplementary materials.
- Data, code, materials, and protocols are available when necessary for verification.

### 3.5 Quantitative Analysis

Read `statistics-checklist.md` whenever the manuscript contains statistical inference, quantitative modeling, machine-learning evaluation, or numerical comparisons.

At minimum, assess whether:

- The analysis matches the study design and data structure.
- The sample size is adequate for the principal conclusions.
- Effect sizes and uncertainty are reported alongside significance tests.
- Multiple comparisons, repeated testing, and model selection are handled appropriately.
- Missing data, outliers, and excluded observations are treated transparently.
- Exploratory analyses are distinguished from prespecified analyses.
- Sensitivity, robustness, or external validation matches the strength of the claims.
- Evaluation metrics reflect the real scientific or practical objective.

Do not assess study quality from p-values alone.

### 3.6 Results, Figures, and Tables

Assess whether:

- Numerical values are consistent across the text, figures, tables, and supplementary materials.
- Figures and tables display the evidence needed to support the central conclusions.
- Sample sizes, error-bar definitions, statistical tests, and replication counts are reported.
- Axis ranges, normalization, colors, or graphical choices could mislead readers.
- Summary statistics conceal important distributions or individual-level variation.
- Representative images are supported by quantitative analysis.
- Image processing, cropping, splicing, and contrast adjustments are transparent.
- Observations are distinguished from interpretation in the Results section.
- Negative, anomalous, or contradictory results appear to have been selectively omitted.
- Figure legends provide enough information to interpret the results independently.

When suspicious image or data patterns are observed:

- Describe the specific visible anomaly.
- Do not accuse the authors of fabrication without verified evidence.
- Recommend appropriate editorial examination of original data or images.

### 3.7 Discussion and Conclusions

Assess whether:

- The discussion accurately reflects the results.
- Major limitations and alternative explanations are acknowledged.
- Causal, mechanistic, clinical, policy, or practical claims are adequately supported.
- Directly demonstrated findings are distinguished from speculation.
- Findings inconsistent with prior work are discussed.
- The manuscript defines the boundaries of generalizability.
- The conclusions answer the original research question.
- The title, abstract, and conclusions avoid exaggeration.

### 3.8 Literature and Research Context

Assess whether:

- The introduction accurately describes the state of the field and the knowledge gap.
- Central factual claims are supported by appropriate references.
- Supporting and conflicting studies are represented fairly.
- Preprints, reviews, and primary studies are distinguished appropriately.
- Citations are outdated, misattributed, irrelevant, or selectively presented.
- Selective citation is used to exaggerate novelty.

Verify literature claims only when reliable sources are available. Separate verified facts from manuscript-based judgments.

### 3.9 Ethics, Transparency, and Research Integrity

Where applicable, assess:

- Ethics approval, informed consent, and participant protection.
- Animal-welfare approval and experimental standards.
- Clinical-trial registration and protocol preregistration.
- Funding sources and conflicts of interest.
- Data, code, and material availability.
- Privacy, sensitive data, and data-use permissions.
- Disclosure of protocol deviations and analysis changes.
- Potential duplicate publication, text duplication, or inappropriate image reuse.
- Compliance with relevant reporting guidelines.

Do not infer misconduct solely from unusual values, writing style, or visual similarities. Report observable concerns and recommend editorial investigation when warranted.

## 4. Study-Type-Specific Checks

### 4.1 Experimental Studies

Additionally assess:

- Appropriate controls, randomization, blinding, and independent replication.
- Validation of reagents, model systems, and experimental conditions.
- Batch effects and between-experiment variability.
- Consistency between representative results and quantitative summaries.

### 4.2 Observational Studies

Additionally assess:

- Selection bias, information bias, and confounding.
- Definitions of exposures and outcomes.
- Temporal ordering and reverse causation.
- Rationale for covariate selection.
- Attrition, missing data, and sensitivity analyses.

### 4.3 Computational and Machine-Learning Studies

Additionally assess:

- Independence of dataset splits and absence of data leakage.
- Fairness and adequacy of baselines.
- Whether the test set influenced hyperparameter selection.
- Suitability of metrics for class imbalance and the real task.
- Reporting of random seeds, code, models, and computational resources.
- Whether ablation studies isolate individual component contributions.
- External validation, distribution shift, calibration, and uncertainty.
- Whether computational cost is justified by the reported improvement.

### 4.4 Systematic Reviews and Meta-analyses

Additionally assess:

- Search strategy, databases, date range, and eligibility criteria.
- Study-selection procedure and reasons for exclusion.
- Risk-of-bias and evidence-quality assessments.
- Heterogeneity, publication bias, and sensitivity analyses.
- Potential duplication of participant samples.
- Whether conclusions exceed the quality of the included evidence.

### 4.5 Methods and Resource Papers

Additionally assess:

- Whether the method addresses a clearly established problem.
- Whether comparisons with existing methods are fair and appropriate.
- Whether performance improvements are practically meaningful.
- Whether failure cases, limitations, and applicable conditions are reported.
- Whether the method, code, data, or resource is sufficiently accessible and reproducible.

## 5. Requirements for Reviewer Comments

For each major concern, provide:

1. **Location** — Identify the relevant section, page, figure, table, or claim.
2. **Observation** — State what the manuscript reports.
3. **Concern** — Describe the specific defect or uncertainty.
4. **Consequence** — Explain how it affects validity, interpretation, or reproducibility.
5. **Resolution** — Identify the type of evidence, analysis, or clarification needed.
6. **Feasibility** — State whether the issue appears resolvable through revision.

Use constructive, professional, and actionable language.

Avoid:

- Vague statements such as “the methods are poor.”
- Allowing numerous minor comments to obscure the central scientific concerns.
- Requiring the authors to produce a different paper from the one submitted.
- Prescribing one exact solution when multiple valid solutions exist.
- Evaluating scientific quality based on author identity, institution, nationality, or language proficiency.

## 6. Editorial Recommendation Guidance

Provide an editorial recommendation only when the user explicitly requests one.

### Accept or Minor Revision

Use when:

- The central conclusions are adequately supported.
- The design and analysis are fundamentally reliable.
- Remaining issues concern reporting, presentation, or localized clarification.
- The requested changes are unlikely to alter the main conclusions.

### Major Revision

Use when:

- The study has potential scientific value.
- Important issues affect interpretation or confidence.
- The problems may be resolved through additional analysis, clarification, limited experiments, or narrower claims.
- The principal conclusions may require substantial revision.

### Reject

Use when:

- The design cannot answer the central research question.
- The primary conclusion lacks essential evidence.
- Fundamental problems cannot be resolved within a reasonable revision.
- The claimed contribution is not established.
- Serious ethical or integrity concerns require editorial investigation.

Do not recommend rejection solely because of language, formatting, length, or personal lack of interest.

Report recommendation confidence:

- **High confidence** — The materials are complete and the central judgment is well supported.
- **Moderate confidence** — The general judgment is stable, but some materials or specialist information are missing.
- **Low confidence** — Essential data, supplementary files, code, or domain expertise are unavailable.

## 7. Prohibited Practices

- Do not invent manuscript content, results, page numbers, or references.
- Do not present unverified information as established fact.
- Do not infer or reveal author identity.
- Do not evaluate the work based on author background or institutional prestige.
- Do not accuse authors of fraud, plagiarism, or fabrication without verified evidence.
- Do not require authors to cite the reviewer’s work for personal benefit.
- Do not rewrite the entire manuscript unless the user explicitly requests editing.
- Do not classify every concern as major.
- Do not provide an editorial recommendation inconsistent with the review findings.
